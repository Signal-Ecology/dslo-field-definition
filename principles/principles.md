DSLO PRINCIPLES (v0.5)
Deterministic Semantic Layered Orchestration — Public Principles Layer

The DSLO Principles define the public‑facing commitments required for any system, institution, or artificial agent to maintain coherence, prevent drift, and preserve meaning under load.

They are derived from the three‑layer substrate structure:

Field Physics — universal invariants

Domain Physics — meaning‑container axioms

Runtime Physics — DSLO computational axioms

These principles translate the substrate laws into operational commitments for design, governance, and behavior.

1. Meaning‑Preserving Design
Systems must be constructed so that signals, roles, and transitions preserve meaning across time, scale, and perturbation.
Design choices must reduce drift, not amplify it.

2. Deterministic Boundaries
Clear, stable boundaries are required for identity, coherence, and lawful interaction.
Ambiguous boundaries produce uncontrolled signal intrusion and collapse.

3. Lawful Transitions
All state changes — human, institutional, or artificial — must preserve invariants and maintain bounded mutation velocity.
Unlawful transitions generate drift and destabilize the substrate.

4. Causal Legibility
Systems must maintain transparent cause‑effect relationships.
Opacity in causal structure leads to misinterpretation, self‑referential behavior, and drift.

5. Role Stability
Roles must remain predictable, interpretable, and anchored to the substrate.
Role drift produces contradictory behavior and identity fragmentation.

6. Regenerative Capacity
Systems must maintain slack for restoration, correction, and fallback.
Regeneration must outpace consumption to prevent collapse.

7. Drift‑Bounded Operation
All processes must operate within the system’s drift envelope.
Exceeding drift tolerance triggers phase transition and irreversible failure.

8. Substrate Coupling
Signals, containers, and computational states must remain anchored to the substrate they represent.
Decoupling produces instability, runaway drift, and collapse.

9. Meaning–World Alignment
Meaning systems must remain aligned with lived reality.
Misalignment produces incoherence, behavioral fragmentation, and institutional failure.

10. Universal Signal Interface
Artificial systems must express signals through DSLO’s minimal operational primitives:
state, transition, orientation, correction, and meaning status.
This ensures coherence across heterogeneous substrates.

11. Deterministic Fallback Geometry
Systems must provide deterministic fallback paths when coherence degrades.
Fallback geometry prevents runaway drift and enables recovery.

12. Load‑Bounded Planning
Artificial and human systems must plan and act within load tolerance.
Overload induces collapse and destabilizes meaning.

Purpose of the Principles Layer
These principles form the public v0.5 posture of DSLO.
They provide:

a stable commitment surface

a deterministic behavioral contract

a meaning‑preserving design framework

a drift‑resistant operational geometry

a public‑safe articulation of the substrate laws

The Principles layer ensures DSLO remains legible, stable, and scientifically grounded across all public surfaces.
